Interseasonal rank mobility in English Premier League 2000-2015

November 20, 2015

Is there any hope for the no-hopers and the strugglers?

Lack of league football over past couple of weekends freed up some time for me to ponder about all the life's existential questions and this was the question that stood out prominently - given today's league standings, how much upward mobility can one expect from their teams? Will Leicester City start sinking down and will Chelsea bubble up? Well, it turns out that while I cannot predict the future, I can certainly look at past. Thanks to the good folks at Whoscored.com, I was able to get all the data I needed to do some analysis. If we divide the 20 teams in the league in groups (or classes) of four each, we can draw parallels with plethora of studies done in socio-economic mobility across the world. In this post, I am going to do a very basic analysis and charting of league mobility using the English Premier league data from 2000-2001 to 2014-2015 season. Any rationale behind choosing first 12 games? Well this is where the table lies at today, so for me it made a good sense to start here.

The first chart below is an attempt at visualizing the upward mobility in league from game 12 to game 38. The two sets of green rectangles represent the league positions after 12 games (the left one) and at the end of the season (the right one). The paths connecting the rectangles from left to right represent the number of instances where a team moved up in ranking in past 15 seasons, the thickness representing the counts of such instances. If you click on any of the rectangles, you will see the related transition data in the adjacent table. You can also see what the paths represent by hovering your mouse over them. Using this visualization, one can find out that Hull City and Middlesbrough had the worst transition, going from 6th to 17th and 8th to 19th respectively in 2008-2009 season. Leicester City went from being 3rd to being 13th in 2000-2001 season, and interestingly they are 3rd at the same stage this time around too.

Visualizing interseason mobility

The next table is based on the study "Intergenerational Social Mobility: The United States in Comparative Perspective" done by Emily Beller and Michael Hout from University of California, Berkley. It shows how often the teams from one class make a transition to the other class. We can clearly see that there has not been a lot of upward mobility in the past. The elites (top 4) stays in the same class for around 78% of time, and for no-hopers (bottom 4), there is no recorded instance of transition to elite class. Based on this table, it seems like Leicester City will do just fine, but its going to take some record breaking endeavor for Chelsea to move to elite class. Anyway, I probably should do some similar analysis for other leagues too in my upcoming post. Maybe they are more mobile compared to their English counterparts.
